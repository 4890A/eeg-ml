Intro

Applications of Machine Learning with Biometrics

Biometrics is a blanket term referring to the statistical analysis of biological data. It is often associated with, but not limited to, the analysis of unique physical or behavioral traits such as DNA, fingerprint or voice patterns. Our research focuses on electroencephalograms (EEG) readings and human activity measured with accelerometer and gyroscope data.

EEGs measure the electrical activity of the brain using electrodes placed on the head. Clinically, EEGs are most often used in the diagnosis of epilepsy, sleep disorders, coma, encephalopathies, and depth of anesthesia. With the rapid advancement of machine learning in the last few decades, researchers have been able to train algorithms to interpret and classify complex EEG signals, opening up many possibilities for the development of mind-controlled prosthetics for amputees, robotic exoskeletons for motor impaired individuals to even the recreation of dreams.

Accelerometer and gyroscopes are devices that are used to determine the position and orientation of an object. In recent years, smartphones and smartwatches have incorporated these devices to allow users to track their daily physical activities and calories burned. By applying machine learning to the data collected by these devices, we're able to classify differnt motion types and apply them to our day to day lives. For example, Apple has refined their watch's algorithm to detect hard falls, if the watch's motion sensors detect that the user is immobile over a short period of time after the event, it will automatically contact emergency services on their behalf.

To demonstrate some of the ways that we can apply machine learning to biometrics, we trained various neural networks on open source data and compared their capacity to classify and predict human conditions. We also attempted to gather raw data via a commercially available EEG, the Muse headband and train a neural network on simple activities.

For an explanation on how machine learning works, we highly recommend checking out 3Blue1Brown's videos on neural networks!

Neurological Impact of Alcoholism


About Models

This data arises from a large study to examine EEG correlation of genetic predisposition to alcoholism. It contains measurements from 64 electrodes placed on subject's scalps which were sampled at 256 Hz (3.9-msec epoch) for 1 second. There were two groups of subjects: alcoholic and control. Each subject was exposed to either a single stimulus (S1) or to two stimuli (S1 and S2) which were pictures of objects chosen from the 1980 Snodgrass and Vanderwart picture set. There were 122 subjects and each subject completed 120 trials where different stimuli were shown. The electrode positions were located at standard sites (Standard Electrode Position Nomenclature, American Electroencephalographic Association 1990). The objective of this project was to develop and test three different predictive models. The models were trained to predict/classify an observation as belonging to a subject identified as “alcoholic” or as a “control” using train data from the 2,830,592 observations of EEG included in the data set.

Classifying Motion



About Phone Watch


Smart phones and watches use gyroscopes and accelerometers to detect motion. We analyzed the HHAR dataset, collected from phones and watches worn by people engaged in activities such as walking, biking, and ascending stairs to see how well the data from those devices predicts the activities. A basic neural network was used first, which predicted activity with an approximate accuracy of 65%, and final obtained a better predictive accuracy of 87% using a Long Short Term Memory (LSTM) network, which is a type of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) which considers not just the current input, but what it remembers about the preceding elements.
